Getting Started

Acts are the fundamental building blocks of your story structure, providing clear narrative progression and pacing control.

Define Act Purpose
Clearly establish what narrative function this act serves - setup, confrontation, or resolution.
Set Story Position
Determine where this act fits in your overall story structure and timeline.
Add Scenes
Create and organize scenes that advance the plot and develop characters.
Review & Refine
Analyze the act's contribution to the overall story and refine as needed.

Story Structure

🎭
Three-Act Structure
The traditional structure works well: Setup (25%), Confrontation (50%), Resolution (25%). Each act should have a clear beginning, middle, and end.
Act I - Setup
Introduce characters, establish the world, and present the inciting incident that launches the story.
Act II - Confrontation
Develop conflict, deepen character relationships, and build tension toward the climax.
Act III - Resolution
Resolve the main conflict, address character arcs, and provide satisfying closure.

Pacing Control

Master the rhythm of your story by controlling tension, action, and emotional beats within each act.

Pacing Techniques:

  • Tension Curves: Build and release tension throughout the act
  • Scene Variety: Alternate between action, dialogue, and introspection
  • Chapter Breaks: Use natural stopping points to control reading flow
  • Emotional Beats: Balance intense moments with quieter character development
  • Cliffhangers: End acts with hooks that pull readers forward
⏱️ Pacing Balance
Not every scene needs high tension. Quiet moments allow readers to process events and connect with characters.

Best Practices

💡 Scene Management
Each scene should advance the plot, develop character, or reveal information. If a scene doesn't serve one of these purposes, consider cutting it.
⚠️ Act Transitions
Make sure each act ends with a clear transition to the next. Readers should feel compelled to continue reading.
🔄 Revision Strategy
Write the full act first, then revise for pacing, character development, and story consistency. Don't get stuck editing individual scenes.
